# Theorem 34: Trade Arrival Rate as Information Proxy

Based on Ane & Geman (2000) and Easley & O'Hara (1992).

## Definitions

Let N(t) be the number of trades in the time interval [0, t]. The trade arrival rate is:

  lambda(t) = dN(t) / dt  (instantaneous rate)

For a fixed-size batch of B trades, the batch duration is D = t_last - t_first. The empirical arrival rate for batch k is:

  R_k = B / D_k

where D_k is the duration of batch k in seconds.

## The Mixture of Distributions Hypothesis (MDH)

Ane & Geman (2000) proved that asset returns are approximately normal when sampled in "transaction time" (number of trades) rather than calendar time. The key result: the number of trades N(t) is a sufficient statistic for the latent information flow rate.

## Claims

1. **Arrival rate as volatility proxy.** Under the MDH, the variance of returns over a calendar time interval [0, t] satisfies:

     Var(r_t) = sigma_trade^2 * E[N(t)]

   where sigma_trade^2 is the per-trade return variance. Prove that higher arrival rate (more trades per unit time) implies higher calendar-time volatility, with the relationship being linear.

2. **Arrival rate and informed trading.** Under Easley & O'Hara (1992), on information event days:
   - Both informed and uninformed traders are active
   - Total arrival rate = lambda_informed + lambda_uninformed > lambda_uninformed (no-event rate)

   Prove that the arrival rate is strictly higher on information event days:

     lambda_event = mu + epsilon_buy + epsilon_sell > epsilon_buy + epsilon_sell = lambda_no_event

   where mu > 0 is the informed trader arrival rate and epsilon are uninformed rates.

3. **Relationship to VPIN.** VPIN estimates P(informed trading) from volume imbalance. Arrival rate provides complementary information:
   - High arrival rate + high VPIN = likely informed event (high toxicity)
   - High arrival rate + low VPIN = likely uninformed activity (volatility without direction)

   Prove that under the Easley-O'Hara model, Corr(arrival_rate, VPIN) > 0 when information events are more common (alpha > 0.5), because information events increase both metrics.

4. **Fixed-batch arrival rate properties.** With batch size B fixed:

     R_k = B / D_k

   Prove that:
   - R_k > 0 always (D_k > 0 for any B >= 1)
   - R_k is inversely proportional to inter-trade duration
   - Var(R) / E[R] > 1 (overdispersion) implies trade clustering beyond a Poisson baseline, which is the same condition used in our hawkes_branching feature (Theorem 11)

   Therefore, trade_arrival_rate and hawkes_branching are derived from the same quantity (R_k) but capture different aspects: the level (R_k) vs the dispersion (Var(R)/E[R]).

5. **Numerical claim.** With B = 100 trades and typical BTC inter-trade time of 10-20ms:
   - D_k ≈ 1-2 seconds
   - R_k ≈ 50-100 trades/second

   During a liquidation cascade: D_k ≈ 0.1-0.5 seconds, R_k ≈ 200-1000 trades/second. The arrival rate increases 5-20x, making it a strong regime indicator.
