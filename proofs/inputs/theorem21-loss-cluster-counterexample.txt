# Theorem 21: LossClusterCounterexample (corrected statement)

Use the standard definitions

  PF      = gross_wins / gross_losses,
  win rate w = wins / total_trades,
  Sortino = mean_return / downside_std * sqrt(m),

where downside_std is the downside semideviation computed from the same return sequence, and m > 0 is steps_per_day.

This theorem asks for a corrected counterexample, because the naive claim

  PF > 1, w > 0.5, but Sortino <= 0

is not compatible with the standard definition of Sortino.

Prove the following claims.

1. Impossibility of the original statement. For any finite return sequence with at least one losing trade and standard Sortino defined from the same sequence, if PF > 1 then the mean return is strictly positive. Therefore Sortino > 0. Conclude that the conjunction

     PF > 1 and Sortino <= 0

   is impossible under the standard definition.

2. Nevertheless, there is no positive lower bound on Sortino in terms of PF > 1 and w > 0.5 alone. For every epsilon > 0, construct a finite return sequence with

     PF > 1,
     w > 1 / 2,
     0 < Sortino < epsilon.

3. Give an explicit six-trade family. Let delta > 0 and consider the return sequence

     (1/2 + delta, 1/2 + delta, 1/2 + delta, 1/2 + delta, -1, -1).

   Prove that

     PF = 1 + 2 * delta,
     w = 2 / 3,
     mean_return = 2 * delta / 3,
     downside_std = 1 / sqrt(3),
     Sortino = (2 / sqrt(3)) * delta * sqrt(m).

   Hence Sortino can be made arbitrarily small and positive by taking delta -> 0+ even though PF > 1 and w > 0.5.

4. Order-independence. Prove that PF, win rate, mean return, and downside semideviation of a finite return sequence depend only on the multiset of returns, not on their temporal order. Conclude that temporal clustering of losses, by itself, does not change the standard Sortino ratio.

5. To make clustering relevant, one must change the objective to a path-dependent one. Give an explicit example of two return sequences with the same multiset of returns but different max drawdown values, for example

     (1, 1, -1, -1)
     and
     (1, -1, 1, -1).

   Prove that they have the same PF, win rate, mean return, and standard trade-level Sortino, but different max drawdown. Conclude that standard Sortino is permutation-invariant, whereas drawdown-based objectives are genuinely path-dependent.
